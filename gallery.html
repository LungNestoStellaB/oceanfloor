Anglerfish

Melanocetus johnsonii

The humpback anglerfish uses bioluminescent lure to attract prey in complete darkness. Females can be 10× larger than males. One of the deep ocean's most iconic predators.

📍 Atlantic / Pacific 🌡 2-4°C ⬇ 200-4,000m

Giant Isopod

Bathynomus giganteus

Related to woodlice and pill bugs, these deep-sea scavengers grow up to 50cm. Can survive years without food. Found on the ocean floor feeding on whale carcasses and dead fish.

📍 Atlantic / Indian 🌡 4°C ⬇ 170-2,140m
600m Mesopelagic [ AWAITING IMAGE ]

Vampire Squid

Vampyroteuthis infernalis

"Vampire squid from hell." Neither squid nor octopus — the sole surviving member of its order. Covered in light-producing organs. Turns inside out when threatened, revealing spines.

📍 Tropical / Temperate 🌡 2-6°C ⬇ 600-1,200m

Giant Tube Worm

Riftia pachyptila

Up to 2.4m tall. No mouth, no stomach, no gut. Relies entirely on chemosynthetic bacteria living inside its body. Grows faster than any known marine invertebrate — up to 85cm per year.

📍 Pacific Vents 🌡 2-30°C ⬇ 2,000-3,000m
8,178m Hadopelagic [ AWAITING IMAGE ]

Mariana Snailfish

Pseudoliparis swirei

The deepest-living fish ever recorded. Found in the Mariana Trench at 8,178m. Translucent, scaleless, small. At this depth, pressure would crush a steel submarine. This fish thrives.

📍 Mariana Trench 🌡 1-2°C ⬇ 6,900-8,200m
10,900m Hadopelagic [ AWAITING IMAGE ]

Challenger Deep Amphipod

Hirondellea gigas

Shrimp-like crustaceans found at the very bottom of the Mariana Trench. Survive pressure of 1,100 atmospheres. Digest wood using enzymes previously unknown to science.

📍 Challenger Deep 🌡 1°C ⬇ 6,000-10,900m
1,500m Bathypelagic [ AWAITING IMAGE ]

Bamboo Coral

Isididae family

Deep-sea corals that grow in segments like bamboo. Some specimens are over 4,000 years old — among the oldest living organisms on Earth. Their growth rings record centuries of ocean chemistry.

📍 Global Deep 🌡 2-4°C ⬇ 200-4,000m
800m Mesopelagic [ AWAITING IMAGE ]

Glass Octopus

Vitreledonella richardi

Almost completely transparent — only the optic nerves, eyes, and digestive tract are visible. A ghost moving through the twilight zone. Rarely photographed alive until deep-sea ROVs.

📍 Tropical / Subtropical 🌡 3-8°C ⬇ 300-1,000m
2,400m Hydrothermal [ AWAITING IMAGE ]

Yeti Crab

Kiwa hirsuta

Discovered 2005. Covered in silky blonde setae (hair-like structures) that harbour chemosynthetic bacteria — it literally farms food on its own body. Waves its arms near hydrothermal vents to feed its bacteria.

📍 Pacific / Antarctic 🌡 2-10°C ⬇ 2,200-2,600m
1,200m Bathypelagic [ AWAITING IMAGE ]

Barreleye Fish

Macropinna microstoma

Transparent dome-shaped head with tubular eyes that rotate inside the skull. Can look straight up through its own head to spot prey silhouettes against the faint surface light above.

📍 North Pacific 🌡 3-6°C ⬇ 600-2,500m
2,800m Hydrothermal [ AWAITING IMAGE ]

Pompeii Worm

Alvinella pompejana

The most heat-tolerant animal on Earth. Lives with its tail in 80°C water and its head in 22°C water — a 60-degree temperature gradient across its 13cm body. Named after Pompeii for surviving extreme heat.

📍 East Pacific Rise 🌡 22-80°C ⬇ 2,500-3,000m
4,000m Abyssopelagic [ AWAITING IMAGE ]

Sea Pig

Scotoplanes globosa

Plump, translucent deep-sea cucumber that walks on the abyssal plains on inflatable tube feet. Often found in herds of hundreds, facing into the current to catch organic particles.

📍 Global Abyssal 🌡 1-4°C ⬇ 1,200-5,000m
